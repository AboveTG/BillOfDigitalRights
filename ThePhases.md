Phase 1: Define the Core Threat

Before writing laws, establish exactly what problem exists.

The strongest formulation I've seen from our discussion is:

Advances in data collection, predictive analytics, artificial intelligence, and persistent surveillance have created the ability to make consequential decisions about individuals based on digital representations of those individuals rather than on their actual conduct.

Notice how this is:

Technology-neutral.
Politically neutral.
Constitutionally grounded.
Difficult to dismiss.

It doesn't attack AI.
It doesn't attack business.
It doesn't attack law enforcement.

It identifies a governance problem.

Phase 2: Choose a Single Foundational Principle

You need one principle that everything else grows from.

Possible candidates:

Option A: Digital Personhood

"Digital representations of individuals deserve legal protection."

Pros:

Philosophically powerful.

Cons:

Legally controversial.
Easy to caricature.
Option B: Cognitive Privacy

"Individuals retain sovereignty over their thoughts and mental processes."

Pros:

Deep constitutional implications.

Cons:

Difficult to define.
Option C: Predictive Sovereignty

"People should not be judged primarily by algorithmic predictions."

Pros:

Highly relevant.
Easy to explain.
Broad appeal.

Cons:

New concept.

Of these, I would start with Predictive Sovereignty.

It is specific enough to legislate and broad enough to grow into a larger doctrine.

Phase 3: Create a Narrow First Proposal

Instead of proposing a Bill of Digital Rights immediately, draft a first act.

The Predictive Due Process Act

Core rule:

No government agency may take adverse action against a person based solely on algorithmic prediction without independent evidence and meaningful human review.

That is easy to defend.

Examples:

A person cannot:

Be investigated solely because an AI predicts criminality.
Lose benefits solely because an AI predicts fraud.
Be denied a license solely because an AI predicts risk.
Be placed on a watchlist solely because an AI predicts future behavior.

This doesn't prohibit AI.

It preserves due process.

Phase 4: Build Constitutional Foundations

Every proposal should tie directly to existing constitutional principles.

Digital Concept	Constitutional Root
Human review	Fifth Amendment Due Process
Explainability	Fourteenth Amendment Due Process
Surveillance limits	Fourth Amendment
Anonymous participation	First Amendment
Protection from predictive punishment	Presumption of Innocence

This is critical.

You're not creating rights.

You're extending existing rights into digital contexts.

Phase 5: Create Definitions

Most legislation succeeds or fails based on definitions.

The next document we should draft is a definitions section for concepts like:

Digital profile
Predictive model
Consequential decision
Automated decision system
Human review
Personal data
Behavioral inference
Government action

These definitions need to be precise enough to survive legal scrutiny but broad enough that agencies can't easily evade them.

Phase 6: Draft the First Actual Bill

Only after the above steps do we write legislative text.

The first bill should be:

3–8 pages.
Narrowly focused.
Bipartisan.
Constitutionally conservative.
Difficult to characterize as anti-technology.
