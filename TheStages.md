Stage 1: Build Credibility

Create three documents:

Problem Statement (1 page)
What is predictive surveillance?
Why existing law is insufficient.
Real-world examples.
Why this affects ordinary citizens.
Legislative Brief (3–5 pages)
Constitutional basis.
Definitions.
Proposed protections.
Expected impacts.
Draft Bill
Actual statutory language.

When you meet with a legislator, the first two documents are usually more important than the third.

Stage 2: Find the Right Issue

A complete Digital Bill of Rights is probably too large for a first proposal.

The issue that appears most politically viable is:

Government agencies should not take adverse action against individuals based solely on algorithmic predictions.

This can be framed as:

Due process.
Government accountability.
Transparency.
Civil liberties.

It avoids becoming a partisan debate about technology.

Stage 3: Build a Minnesota Pilot

Minnesota is actually a reasonable place to start because state legislatures can move faster than Congress.

Instead of:

"Let's create a national Digital Bill of Rights."

Start with:

"Let's establish digital due process protections for Minnesotans."

A successful state law becomes:

A proof of concept.
A case study.
A model bill for other states.
Stage 4: Anticipate Opposition

The first questions you'll likely get are:

"What specific harm are we preventing?"
"What existing laws fail to address this?"
"Does this impede law enforcement?"
"Does this hinder AI innovation?"
"What are the compliance costs?"
"How do we define an algorithm?"

If you cannot answer those clearly, the proposal will stall regardless of its merits.

Stage 5: Build the Foundation First

Before scheduling meetings, I would draft:

Congressional/Legislative Findings
Definitions Section
Predictive Due Process Act (Minnesota Version)

Those three documents are the foundation. Once they are solid, you can start approaching state representatives, state senators, committee staff, and policy advisors near the Capitol.

The key is to avoid presenting this as a fight against technology. The strongest framing is:

Constitutional rights should remain effective even when decisions are increasingly informed by data, algorithms, and predictive systems.

That is a position many people across the political spectrum can engage with.
